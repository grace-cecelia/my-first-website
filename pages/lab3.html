Lab 3: Storyboarding for non-linear media

January 22, 2026

Exercise

Description

    1. Reverse engineer a Pokemon battle gameplay recording.

    2. Create storyboards describing the sequence of events and player interactions.

    3. Swap storyboards with a partner and compare your solutions.

My Storyboards

Reflection

Partner Comparison

Partner: Vicky

Vicky's storyboards were more visually based with less accompanying text. I focused on describing each action in sequence within one panel, with up to 6 or 7 actions per panel. Most of Vicky's panels contained only one action and one player interaction, leading to a cleaner sequence. Despite these differences, we used similar techniques for showing motion and camera actions.

I think Vicky's integration of player interactions was simpler and more elegant than mine. I'm not sure it was necessary for me to include every single stepwise action and interaction, and I could have combined some of the steps into one. Vicky's storyboards are cleaner and simpler to read than mine, even though they have less detail overall.

Linear vs. non-linear

This exercise showed me the complexity of storyboarding for non-linear media. Storyboards for any project can be complex, with many elements to consider and work out, but non-linear media involves the extra element of user actions. I think a big challenge I faced was not just depicting this gameplay sequence as if it were an animation and instead thinking about how to connect the player's actions with the actions occurring in the game.

I used many of the same techniques for these storyboards that I've used to storyboard animations in the past, as the visual language for both animations and interactive media can have a lot of overlap. However, while animations traditionally play independently of audience interactions, the driving force behind non-linear media like games is the user's actions. This means that there are many more possibilities within non-linear media that need to be considered, and there are many more design choices we have to make to ensure that our audience's needs are met. While audience members can passively watch an animation and absorb the information it contains, gaining that same information in an interactive format requires a design that facilitates specific types, orders, and levels of interaction.
